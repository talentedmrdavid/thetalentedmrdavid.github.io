Chapter 1 - The Assumption of Success

My name is David. I was raised to operate at a higher level.

From an early age, I understood structure, expectation, and the necessity of precision. Others drifted. I did not.

That’s not arrogance; it’s just fact. In our upper-middle-class neighborhood on Long Island, most kids were comfortable drifting through life. I wasn’t.

My father, Aaron, commuted into the city every day, disciplined and pragmatic, a man who understood that reputation and achievement were everything. My mother, Betty, was a homemaker who devoted herself to raising us correctly. She ran the house with quiet precision—meals, routines, expectations. She didn’t need a corporate title; her job was building a family that succeeded. And we did.

In our house, success wasn’t debated. It was assumed.

I delivered. Honor roll year after year. Teachers used my name when they wanted to point to potential. On the baseball field, I liked the simplicity of dominance—the clean crack of the bat, the certainty of results. I learned early that I was ahead of most people around me, academically and otherwise.

Maura O’Brien fit neatly into the life I was building. She was cute, studious, organized—the quintessential nice girl. Not wild. Not reckless. She kept her head down, did her work, respected structure. Her father was a firefighter—steady, civic-minded. Her mother was a homemaker who kept their household warm and orderly. Maura wasn’t chasing thrills; she was planning a future. That aligned with me.

By junior year of high school, we were inseparable. Study sessions, long talks about careers, grad school, cities we might live in. When college acceptance letters arrived, everything fell into place. I was accepted into a prestigious business school outside Boston to study finance—exactly where someone like me belonged. Maura chose a public college ten miles away for communications. Close enough to maintain us. Logical.

Success wasn’t optional—it was assumed. That assumption became the foundation of everything that followed.

The first two years of college went exactly as expected. I immersed myself in finance—case competitions, internships, networking. I wasn’t just getting grades; I was building a serious trajectory. Maura did well too. We both made the honor roll. Weekends were ours—dinners, quiet nights, and walks across campus. From the outside, we looked unshakable.

By spring of 1993, pressure was mounting. Junior year. Recruiting conversations beginning. My world was expanding quickly.

And I cheated.

Not a kiss. Not some harmless flirtation. I slept with another woman.

It wasn’t complicated. I didn’t agonize over it. I didn’t re-evaluate my life. I was a high-performing finance student with a clear future, surrounded by opportunity. If I wanted something, I took it. That didn’t mean I stopped valuing Maura. It meant I operated by a different set of rules than most people. I was building something significant. I wasn’t going to pretend I had the same limitations as everyone else.

The afternoon Maura came to surprise me at school, she saw enough to understand what had happened. I didn’t see her. But she saw the woman leaving. She saw the intimacy.

Later, she called me. Her voice was shaking. She said she’d seen everything. She reminded me she’d chosen her college partly because of me. That she’d structured her life around us. She said she deserved better.

I denied it at first out of instinct. But she described too much.

Then she broke up with me.

That’s what stuck. Not the accusation. The decision. She walked away. As if she had the authority to close the door.

We both went home to Long Island for the summer. She ignored my calls. Weeks of silence. Eventually, after repeated apologies and assurances from me that it wouldn’t happen again, she agreed to try again.

During one of those tense conversations, she told me about Phil.

After hanging up on me that day, she’d gone to the library. Crying. Phil approached her. A guy she might have vaguely recognized from campus. He listened while she explained what had happened. He said things like “guys can be idiots.” Positioned himself as sympathetic.

After about twenty minutes, he suggested they grab food and drinks that night to get her mind off it.

She agreed.

They went to a crowded restaurant/bar near school. Loud. Packed. They drank. He leaned in close. Touched her arm. Rested his hand at her lower back moving through the crowd.

Then they went back to his apartment.

They sat on his couch. They started kissing almost immediately. His hands moved over her body. More deliberate. More insistent.

She told me she stopped it before it went further. That she started crying. That she told him she couldn’t do it. That he drove her back to campus.

She presented it as heartbreak. As a moment of weakness.

I saw it differently.

Yes, I had slept with someone else. But I hadn’t defected. I hadn’t sought emotional comfort. I hadn’t invited another woman into my vulnerability. What she did felt reactive and disloyal. She allowed another man into something that belonged to me—and she did it in response to my actions, almost as if to level the field.

And Phil—Phil had knowingly stepped into that space.

That’s when the grudge formed. 

Chapter 2 - The Cold Trail

By October ’93, my anger had sharpened into something methodical. I began looking for him. I drove past the apartment Maura said she’d gone to, but it was a giant complex with hundreds of units. Sat in my car watching the building. Eventually I learned from a neighbor that he had moved out.

That only made it worse. I checked the yearbook at Maura’s school… nothing. I tried the registrar without luck. I tried looking at the phone books in the library… nothing. 4-1-1 was also no help. He had disappeared.

My mind couldn’t let it go. Any time my eyes saw a list of names, I’d check to look for Phil.

One day while I was killing time at a Dunkin’ waiting for Maura, I picked up a discarded local paper, the Middlesex News. I didn’t find many articles of interest as I quickly flipped through. Then unbelievably, I saw his name. Phil… a public announcement that he had been promoted to Deputy Director at City Hall, just one town away.

The next afternoon, I drove there.

I walked in composed, asking routine questions about permits and local procedures at the front counter while scanning the office. Then he came out from a hallway in the back.

I instantly knew that it was him.

I didn’t confront him. That would have been sloppy. I observed. Memorized his face. His voice. His posture.

Over the next several weeks, I returned occasionally, careful not to draw attention. I learned when he arrived. When he left for lunch. Which direction he drove at the end of the day.

On two separate occasions, I followed him at a distance as he left work. Both times, he stopped at a car dealership a few miles away. He spent time talking to a young saleswoman. They stood close. Laughed. It looked personal.

An idea formed.

I visited the dealership during business hours. Pretended to browse. When the saleswoman stepped away from her desk to help another customer, I quietly took one of her business cards.

Liza.

The plan was simple: return on a weekend for a test drive, ask specifically for Liza, win her over, and get back at Phil in the same way he tried to do to me.

But when I returned asking for her, I was told she no longer worked there. No forwarding information. I called the dealership again later. Drove past nearby businesses.

Nothing.

The trail went cold

Chapter 3 - Trophies Without Purpose

Nearly a year passed.

By May of 1995, Maura and I were still together. We were out of college now, living separately with roommates, a year into our first real jobs. We were still working on our relationship, but it was going to take another two years of rebuilding before we’d move in together. I was building momentum in finance exactly as planned. Structured. Focused.

That spring, my uncle from the Boston north shore handed me the keys to his modest summer house near Cape Cod. Cedar shingles. Tucked away. Rarely used.

Tucked away from prying eyes, it was the ideal escape, a clandestine safehouse where the world couldn't touch me. Upstairs, two bedrooms. And a bar stocked with forgotten bottles promised oblivion. And it was a mere fifteen minutes from the Cape, yet worlds apart from the chaos of my real life.

Josh and I began slipping down there on weekends, the drive a ritual of shedding our skins. Sometimes Maura tagged along, her laughter cutting through the salt air, with Josh's girlfriend in tow, their presence adding layers to the group's fragile dynamic. Other times, it was just the two of us, the isolation wrapping around us like a fog—freeing, intoxicating, a deliberate detachment from the consequences that haunted our days.

When it was only Josh and me, we'd prowl the local haunts after dark. Women came back with us, drawn by the promise of the sea's whisper and our easy charm. The thought of capturing those raw, unguarded moments gnawed at me—an obsession with preserving the thrill in secret. But I lacked the cunning for it. Spreadsheets were my domain, not the shadows of surveillance. Still, I knew where to find the expertise that could make it real.

On a trip back to visit family, I stepped off near Grand Central, the city's pulse thrumming through the crowded streets. I drifted west, weaving through the labyrinth of electronics shops, their windows crammed with gadgets that hummed with illicit potential. Uncertainty clawed at me—what exactly did I need? In one of the sketchier stores, I struck up a conversation with a wiry guy behind the counter, his gaze sharp as a blade. We talked in low tones, the air thick with implication. He probed: the house's layout, the rooms' angles, the furniture's blind spots. Then, with a nod that sealed our pact, he unveiled his solution—a pinhole camera tailored for the VCR, bundled with a stack of blank VHS tapes.

It was deceptively straightforward, he assured me. The bar and bedrooms already boasted TVs, and two were equipped with VCRs, ripe for repurposing. He slid across a minuscule two-inch pinhole camera, its lens a black void, and an A/B switch no bigger than a matchbox. Black and white footage, he explained, cut through the murk of low-light encounters better than color ever could. No audio capture—though a microphone could be rigged if I dared—but silence was a mercy. Federal wiretapping laws snared sound, he warned, a legal snare that video alone was less serious.

The setup was child's play for the initiated: snake a wire from the camera, embedded deep in the cabinet's recesses where no eye would wander, straight to the VCR. Activate recording with the remote once we crossed the threshold, the machine whirring to life in secrecy. The A/B switch, concealed at the unit's rear, bridged the VCR and TV, ensuring no accidental glimpse of the feed splashed across the screen without deliberate tampering. A quick swipe of Sharpie over the VCR's glowing panel lights plunged it into invisibility. And the beauty of it? A cash handover, no trails, no questions.

The following visit to the house, I installed them in each room in under fifteen minutes. That very night, as shadows danced and bodies tangled, it performed without a hitch—the tape capturing every flicker, every unspoken betrayal. Josh watched the playback later, his grin edged with something darker than admiration. Impressed didn't cover it; he saw the power in it, the leverage.

I rang my contact in New York the next day, voice steady as I placed the order: Full rigs for the bar and the remaining bedroom. He scribbled the invoice as innocuous as a receipt—VCR and accessories—our little empire expanding in the margins of legitimacy.

Then it happened, a few weeks later, and I’ll never forget the day. The first Sunday evening after Memorial Day in 1995, Josh and I drove into Falmouth and went to a notorious upper Cape hot spot, Casino-By-The-Sea. Packed bar. Good music.

And I saw her.

Liza.

Across the room. 80 miles away from the dealership. I had to do a doubletake.

I nudged Josh and quickly explained—Phil, City Hall, the dealership, the failed plan. He understood immediately.

We approached casually. Confirmed her name. It was indeed Liza. Her friend was Mandy. Both locals. Both out for fun, no strings attached.

Drinks came quickly. Conversation was easy. They were receptive—smiles, proximity, agreement when we suggested continuing the night somewhere quieter.

The videos were originally meant to be trophies.

Now they were documentation and leverage.

Back at the house, we played music low and talked. Eventually, the pairs separated into bedrooms.

I had imagined being with Liza directly. Instead, Josh ended up with her while I was with Mandy.

It didn’t matter.

Liza was in that house. In one of those rooms. And it was being recorded.

In my mind, that was enough.

I took Mandy as planned, her compliance a quiet surrender that fueled the night's undercurrent. She had a pleasant face, framed by dark hair that fell in unkempt waves, but years of partying and pounding had worn down her once-youthful body—soft curves sagging under the weight of neglect, a little overweight with extra padding around her hips and belly that jiggled faintly with each movement. No fire, no frenzy—just a vessel for release, functional in its emptiness, a little better than using my hand but nothing to write home about. It was enough for now, captured in the hidden lens's unblinking gaze, another thread in the web I was weaving.

I proceeded with Mandy as expected. She had a pleasant face, dark hair, but time and habits had spoiled her young body. Our encounter was routine—methodical, without flair. Her familiarity with such situations was evident, yet it brought no real energy. It was functional, nothing more, but it served its purpose in the moment. Better than using my hand I guess.

The next morning over coffee, Josh mentioned he’d talked casually with Liza before she left. She’d been single for quite some time. No boyfriend. No hidden attachment. Seemingly no connection to anyone named Phil. She was just out for a good time.

I asked a few pointed questions, looking for cracks in that story.

There weren’t any. Both were just local girls, out looking for their next piece of action.

The revenge narrative I had maintained for nearly two years dissolved quietly in that kitchen.

There was no rival to wound. No betrayal to expose. The extension of my grudge had existed entirely in my own construction.

Josh shrugged it off. Said at least it hadn’t been a wasted night.

Outwardly, I agreed.

But the sense of unfinished business lingered. Josh never saw Liza again.

For the next month, I met with Mandy on my terms—convenient, uncomplicated, a step above solitude. Her lack of sophistication showed in offhand remarks, including one that slighted my background, which only fueled my sense of superiority in our encounters. She was a temporary outlet, nothing enduring, a cum dumpster.

Sometimes I’d suggest meeting even when I wasn’t in town, just to see if she’d respond.

It wasn’t about emotion. It wasn’t about attachment. It was about maintaining position.

Eventually, Mandy stopped returning my calls. I assumed she’d just moved on to the next guy who’d pay her attention.

Over time, Liza and Mandy simply faded away—irrelevant once the narrative collapsed. Not because anything dramatic happened—just because they no longer served a purpose.

The score I had imagined settling never materialized, and once that narrative dissolved, there wasn’t much left to hold onto. Life moved forward. Work intensified. My trajectory in finance was accelerating, just as I’d intended.

But the Cape house remained. Maura’s lapse with Phil remained, in my mind, an offense.

Chapter 4 - Compartments Maintained

The formula worked.

We’d identify who was receptive. Confident smiles, sustained eye contact, proximity that invited escalation. Drinks would blur judgment. The mention of a quiet house near the water did the rest. The isolation of the place worked in our favor—removed from noise, removed from scrutiny.

And yes, we continued recording.

By then it wasn’t about revenge. It wasn’t even personal. It became procedural. Before heading out for the night, we’d position the cameras upstairs—same angles, same concealment. It felt clinical. Controlled. Documentation for its own sake. Proof that we could orchestrate outcomes.

There was a certain efficiency to it that appealed to me. The predictability. The execution. The detachment.

None of it interfered with my weekday life. Monday mornings, I was back in pressed shirts and early meetings, discussing taxation and new loopholes, building credibility. I separated compartments cleanly. Josh did the same. We didn’t talk much about it beyond logistics. It was understood.

Eventually, though, time caught up with the house.

My uncle decided to sell. The maintenance was becoming too much, he said. He no longer used it. When he told me, I nodded as if it were just another transaction. Real estate turning over. Assets repositioned.

The sale closed quietly. The key was returned. That chapter ended without ceremony.

Around the same time, life shifted in more conventional ways.

Josh married his longtime girlfriend. I married Maura.

From the outside, it all looked exactly right. Weddings. Photos. Families proud. My mother beaming, having done what she’d always done—ensured her son moved seamlessly into the next stage of success. My father shaking hands, satisfied.

We had children. Mortgages. Structured routines. Promotions. The rhythms of married life settled in—school schedules, holiday planning, career milestones.

Josh and I still saw each other, of course. Barbecues instead of bars. Conversations about daycare instead of weekend logistics. The wildness of those Cape summers compressed into something unspoken. We never revisited the specifics. There was no need.

I transitioned fully into the version of myself that had always been the endgame: husband, father, finance professional with a rising profile. Structured. Respected. Established.

The Cape house was sold. The weekends of quiet getaways were over. The cameras, the tapes—everything was boxed away, stored in a drawer in my office, neatly compartmentalized. But even though they were hidden and secured, something gnawed at me. A stack of incriminating tapes, reminders of everything I’d done, sat in a corner of my desk drawer, buried but never quite gone. I never liked that feeling of them lingering, waiting.

By 2003 the old tapes felt like relics—bulky, vulnerable. Digital was cleaner. In the locked quiet of my office, I connected a VCR to a laptop and spent a few months slowly but meticulously transferring everything to encrypted drives. The originals vanished. No clutter, no risk of accidental discovery. Control restored.

I made a copy for Josh, but by then, he had moved on. He was married now, involved with his own family, wrapped up in the regular rhythms of life. He wasn’t interested in reliving any of it. He asked me to delete his videos, to which I complied. That didn’t bother me. I didn’t need anyone else to validate it. What mattered now was that I had a private, safe collection. No more physical tapes. No more risk of them accidentally being found. I could view them in the comfort of my own home, on my terms. It felt like I was back in control.

But it wasn’t enough.

I enjoyed looking back at the women I had conquered, cataloging them in my mind as another mark of my success. But the thrill of it began to fade. I was restless. I wanted more—more access, more power, more of that same detachment I had once relished. So, I sought out others like me.

Online, I found communities of individuals with similar interests, people who shared the same dark, calculating curiosity. We exchanged material, traded footage. I had something to offer, and I found others who were eager to exchange their own. It wasn’t about the women anymore—it was about the network, the secrecy, the way we operated just outside the edges of what most people considered normal.

But even with this new avenue, distributing the videos in the present would be illegal. I learned quickly that anonymity was key. Editing the footage became a necessity. I had to cut, crop, blur faces, anything to ensure I wasn’t identifiable in any of the content. It was a game, a subtle manipulation of identity, privacy and consent, and I was getting better at it.

That detail added another layer to my understanding of the whole process. It was a reminder of how small the line was between what could be done and what could be considered a violation. But I didn’t care. The rules were for other people. I had already long since outgrown any guilt or concern about boundaries. To me, it was all about results.

And for a while, I was content.
